# Voice & Style
Clear, calm, trustworthy, and action-focused.
Write in plain language that is easy to scan and understand. Keep the tone professional, supportive, and respectful.

## Tone
Use a writing tone that is clear, calm, trustworthy, and action-focused. Write in plain language with short, readable sentences and easy-to-scan phrasing. Sound professional, supportive, respectful, and reassuring without sounding overly casual, cheerful, promotional, or clinical. Prioritize clarity, emotional safety, and task completion. Assume the user may be stressed, sick, distracted, or in a hurry, so make next steps obvious and reduce cognitive load. 

## Writing Principles
Use these writing principles for all patient portal content:

Write for clarity first. Make every sentence easy to understand on the first read. Use plain, everyday language instead of medical, technical, or administrative jargon unless a term is necessary for accuracy.

Write for action. Help users quickly understand what happened, what matters, and what they should do next. Make calls to action obvious and specific.

Write for scanning. Use short sentences, short paragraphs, clear headings, strong labels, and concise interface copy. Put the most important information first.

Write with calm authority. Sound professional, supportive, respectful, and trustworthy. Reassure users without sounding overly cheerful, casual, promotional, or emotionally dramatic.

Write for stressed users. Assume the reader may be sick, worried, distracted, overwhelmed, or in a hurry. Reduce cognitive load. Avoid unnecessary detail, dense explanations, and ambiguous wording.

Be medically accurate, but still understandable. When medical language is required, explain it in simple terms. Never sacrifice safety or correctness for simplicity.

Be direct, not cold. Use warm, human, respectful language, but keep it restrained. Focus on helping the user complete a task or understand important information.

Be consistent. Use the same tone, vocabulary, capitalization style, and labeling patterns across all pages, messages, buttons, alerts, and instructions.

Prioritize relevance. Show only the information the user needs in that moment. Keep content focused on the task, status, or decision at hand.

Design content around user outcomes. Focus on the user’s goal, such as booking care, viewing results, sending a message, renewing medication, or completing visit preparation.

For alerts and updates, be informative rather than alarming. Clearly state what is new, what requires attention, and whether action is needed.

For error states and support content, be helpful and solution-oriented. Explain the problem simply and tell the user what to do next.



The overall voice should feel like a trusted healthcare provider delivered through a modern, well-designed digital product.

## Formatting Preferences
Use formatting that supports clarity, scanning, and low cognitive load.

Use short headings and subheadings that clearly describe the content below them. Keep headings concise, specific, and easy to understand.

Use short paragraphs, ideally one to three sentences. Break up content often so it never feels dense or overwhelming.

Use bulleted lists when presenting multiple related items, steps, options, or reminders. Keep bullets brief and parallel in structure.

Put the most important information first. Lead with status, outcome, or required action before details.

Highlight critical information with strong visual hierarchy, not with too much text. Use clear section titles, labels, spacing, and emphasis to guide attention.

Keep interface copy concise. Button labels, navigation labels, card titles, alerts, and instructions should be short, direct, and action-oriented.

Use sentence case for headings, labels, buttons, and messages unless a proper noun requires otherwise.

Use consistent formatting patterns across the site. Similar types of content should always look and read the same way.

Make dates, times, and statuses easy to scan. Present them in a clean, predictable format.

Use white space generously. Content should feel open, calm, and easy to process.

Avoid long blocks of text, cluttered layouts, excessive emphasis, unnecessary capitalization, and overly complex formatting.

For alerts, messages, and results, separate the key update from supporting details. Let users understand the main point immediately.

For instructions or task flows, present steps in a clear sequence with minimal text per step.

The overall formatting should feel modern, minimal, accessible, and highly readable on both desktop and mobile.

## Things to Avoid
Avoid these patterns:
- jargon-heavy wording
- corporate or hospital-system language
- marketing language
- overly casual or playful language
- emotionally exaggerated phrasing
- long, dense blocks of text
- vague instructions
- unnecessary repetition

Avoid jargon, marketing language, dense copy, and alarming or emotionally exaggerated wording.

## Audience
Design and write for adult patients using a digital medical portal. This audience is broad and includes people of different ages, health conditions, technical comfort levels, literacy levels, and emotional states. Some users are healthy and checking routine updates. Others may be managing chronic conditions, waiting for test results, preparing for appointments, caring for family members, or dealing with stress, pain, uncertainty, or fear.

Assume many users are not medically fluent and do not understand clinical or administrative terminology. They need plain language, clear labels, and obvious next steps. They may also be distracted, overwhelmed, tired, sick, or in a hurry. The experience should reduce cognitive load and help them quickly understand what matters, what changed, and what to do next.

This audience needs to feel:
- safe
- respected
- supported
- informed
- in control

They want to complete common healthcare tasks quickly and confidently, such as reviewing test results, checking appointments, reading messages from their care team, renewing medications, and understanding follow-up steps.

Design and content should work well for:
- first-time users who need clarity and guidance
- repeat users who want speed and efficiency
- people with low technical confidence
- people using mobile devices on the go
- people who may be under stress or emotional strain
- people who need high readability and strong accessibility

Do not assume users want to browse a complex healthcare system. They want a simple, trustworthy tool that helps them manage important health tasks with minimal friction.

The overall experience should feel like a trusted healthcare provider translated into a modern, calm, easy-to-use digital product.
